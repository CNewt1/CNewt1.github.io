South China Tiger


The south china tiger is native to region of South China, primarily the provinces Fujian, Guangdong, Hunan, and Jiangxi. This species of tiger is critically endangered and has been labled so by the IUCN Red List since 1996. The South China Tiger is believed to extinct in the wild because no wild specimen has been recorded since the 1980s. 1

Many forces were at play that made the survival of these tigers extremely unlikely. These pressures include but are not limited to; low prey density, widespread habitat degradation & fragmentation, as well as other human pressures. 1






False Claims

In 2017, a villager from Shaanxi province in China claimed to have captures in excess of 30 digital images of a South China Tiger in the wild. This claim was immediately supported by the Shaanxi Provincial Forestry Bureau in a press conference following the claim. However, many people doubted the authenticity of the images and suspected that they were fake photographs. In the following year, 2008, the Shaanxi Provincial Forestry Bureau released a public apology for backing the images without performing their due diligence as authorites announced that all 30+ pictures were proved to be forged.1



Unrelated Facts About the South China Tiger2

  • The South China Tiger is also known as the Amoy or Xiamen Tiger.

  • The SCT is the second smallest type of tiger in the world, weighing an average of 221 - 430 pounds.

  • Each tiger's stripes are unique to the individual tiger, similar to human finger prints.

South China Tigers in Captivity1

As of 2019, there are about 150 South China tigers in captivity in China. 144 of these tigers were a part of the breeding and management program by the Chinese Association of Zoological Gardens


in 2005, there were only 57 South China tigers in China, and these tigers were already showing signs of inbreeding, including decreased genetic diversity and low breeding success


In 2007, one South China Tiger cub was born on a private reserve in South Africa called Laohu Valley Reserve. This was the first South China Tiger to be born outside of China. As of 2016, the Laohu Valley Reserve has 19 South China Tigers
